# What Do You Want to Be When You Grow Up?

Someone in an interview asked me that. I'm running a govtech company and a state rep campaign in the 2nd Worcester District at the same time, I've got an MPA and a MCPPO and fifteen years of municipal scars, and they hit me with the kindergarten question.

I didn't have a good answer when I was five, either.

I never wanted to be a firefighter or an astronaut. I wanted to understand why things worked the way they did — and why, most of the time, they didn't. I was the kid who wanted to know who decided where the stop sign went. Not the engineer who designed it. The person who said *here*.

I fell into municipal government the way most people do: by showing up. I raised my hand for a committee. Then I was a City Councillor. Then a Town Administrator. And in every role, in every town, I saw the same thing: good people drowning in bad process. A resident calls about a permit and gets transferred three times. A department head stays late because the approval chain lives in somebody's head and that somebody's on vacation. A clerk keeps a spreadsheet that is, functionally, the law — and nobody knows it exists until she retires. The names change. The org charts change. The problem never does.

We ask people to carry loads the structure should carry. Then we blame them when they drop it.

That's what I wanted to be when I grew up, I just didn't have the language for it yet. I wanted to be the person who fixes the invisible machinery — not the trucks and pipes and roads, but the rules about who can approve what, the process that determines whether the thing you need actually reaches the person who can do something about it.

I wrote a framework called VAULT. Verification, Authority, Utility, Legitimacy, Transfer. Five conditions that have to be true before a government action should happen. I knew it was right the way you know load-bearing walls — you feel them. But it was just a framework on paper until my co-founder Allison — Dr. Allison Weiss Rothschild, organizational psychologist — made it real. She sees the behavioral fault lines I'd walk right past. I wrote the doctrine. She turned it into something that actually changes how people work.

So we built it. PuddleJumper. The first governance process runtime — a layer that sits between a decision and an action, evaluates authority, routes to the right person, and produces an audit trail that can't be altered. SQLite triggers enforce it. You can't bypass the record. It's not a feature. It's the environment.

The logo is a duck in a WWII fighter plane. Calm on the surface, governance machinery underneath. That's the whole pitch.

We're live in Phillipston. We're deploying in Sutton and Westminster on Community Compact IT grants. We're running NMTC compliance for a ten-million-dollar biochar deal. I wrote the code myself. I wrote the grant applications myself. I write everything myself because if you can't explain it in plain language, you don't understand it well enough to build it.

And now I'm running for State Rep — Gardner, Ashburnham, Templeton, Winchendon — because I learned something in all those years: you can write the best software in the world and it still can't fix a statute. Some problems need someone in the room where the law gets made who's actually sat in the chair where the law gets applied.

So what do I want to be when I grow up?

The same thing I've always wanted to be. The person who makes the thing work the way it was supposed to work in the first place — and who's honest about the fact that right now, it doesn't.
