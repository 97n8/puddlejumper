# Town Administrator Interview Bank — 90 Questions with Model Answers

Organized by competency. Each question gives the **trap** (what weak candidates do), the **model
answer spine**, and where applicable a **STAR** scaffold. Drill until the spines are reflex, then make
the stories your own. Citations point to `WORKBOOK.md` modules.

> Scoring guide for self-practice: rate each delivered answer 1–5 on (a) statutory accuracy,
> (b) board-relationship instinct, (c) specificity/examples, (d) composure. Anything ≤3 goes back into
> rotation.

## A. Role, authority & governance (M1–M2)

**A1. What's the difference between a town administrator and a town manager in New Hampshire?**
*Trap:* treating them as synonyms. *Spine:* Manager = statutory office (RSA 37), administrative head
of departments with delegated statutory powers; Administrator = no independent statute, board retains
all RSA 41 authority, you act by delegation. Close with: "I serve the board; I make their authority
effective."

**A2. Who do you work for?** The board **as a body**, by majority vote — not any single member.

**A3. Which town officials report to you, and which don't?** Appointed dept heads typically do;
**elected** clerk/tax collector/moderator/trustees do **not**. Know the line before day one.

**A4. A selectman calls you nightly with directives the other two haven't heard. How do you handle
it?** Take the input, but act only on board majority; keep all three equally informed; gently route
decisions back to the full board. "No surprises, one voice."

**A5. What is the board's role vs. yours?** Policy and appropriation authority is theirs; execution and
administration is mine. I bring options and a recommendation; they decide; I implement.

**A6. What can a town meeting do that the board cannot?** Appropriate money, bond (3/5), adopt
bylaws/ordinances, elect officers — legislative powers reserved to the voters.

**A7. What's the role of the budget committee, if the town has one?** Independent body that prepares
(RSA 32:14–24, MS-737) or recommends the budget; I staff it with accurate data but never try to
control it.

**A8. How do you work with quasi-judicial boards (Planning, ZBA)?** Staff and counsel support only;
never lobby; protect the record and due process.

**A9. What's the moderator's role and how do you coordinate?** They run the meeting/deliberative
session and rule on procedure; I make sure the warrant, budget, and logistics are airtight so they can.

**A10. When do you call town counsel vs. handle it yourself?** Anything with litigation, Right-to-Know
gray areas, personnel termination, land/contract risk, or statutory ambiguity. Knowing the edge of my
competence is the job.

## B. Finance & budgeting (M3)

**B1. Walk us through building next year's budget.** Department requests → reconcile to CIP and
revenue forecast → board priorities → draft on MS-636/MS-737 → calculate default budget (SB2) →
public hearing (RSA 32:5 / 40:13 II-a) → post with warrant (RSA 31:95) → deliberative session → ballot
→ MS-232 within 20 days → DRA sets rate.

**B2. What is RSA 32 and why does it matter?** The Municipal Budget Law: appropriation by purpose,
expenditure limits (32:8), transfers (32:10), lapse (32:7), emergency over-expenditure with DRA
approval (32:11). It's the legal frame for every dollar.

**B3. What's the difference between MS-636 and MS-737?** 636 = towns without an official budget
committee; 737 = towns with one (certified by committee majority, filed within 20 days of meeting).

**B4. What's an appropriate unassigned fund balance?** Roughly 5–17% of operating budget per DRA
guidance; I'd set a target with the board (cash flow + bond rating + emergency cushion) and manage it
as a multi-year smoothing tool, not a one-year tax cut.

**B5. The board wants to apply $1M of fund balance to cut taxes this year. Your counsel?** Show the
three-year trajectory: lower rate now, but a cushion drawdown and likely a sharper increase next year.
Give them the tradeoff with numbers; the decision is theirs, made with eyes open.

**B6. Explain transfers between appropriations.** Under RSA 32:10 the governing body may transfer
between purposes within the total appropriation unless the meeting voted line-item — documented, with
the board informed.

**B7. What happens to unspent appropriations at year end?** They lapse (RSA 32:7) unless properly
encumbered or voted non-lapsing.

**B8. How do you fund a $1.2M public-works facility?** Don't cold-vote a bond. Build it into the CIP,
pre-fund a capital reserve (RSA 35) over several years to shrink the bond, run public info sessions
before the deliberative session, then bring a smaller, well-understood number. (Swanzey's failed 3/5
vote, 681–485, is exactly the cautionary case.)

**B9. What's a CIP and why does it matter?** A rolling 6+-year capital plan (RSA 674:5–8) that prevents
surprise capital from spiking the rate and sequences big asks. I'd keep it live and tied to the budget.

**B10. How does bonding work?** Long-term debt under RSA 33 generally needs a 3/5 meeting vote; I
prepare the case, manage bond counsel/financial advisor, and watch debt-service-to-budget and the
rating impact.

**B11. What drives the property tax rate?** Four DRA-combined components: municipal + county + state
education (SWEPT) + local education. Education is ~60%+ and outside town control — I'd never let a
board think I can wave away the school number.

**B12. A department overspends its line. What do you do?** Stop it (RSA 32:8 prohibits over-
expenditure), find the cause, transfer within total if lawful (32:10), and only as a true emergency use
the RSA 32:11 DRA-approved process. Then fix the controls.

**B13. How do you forecast revenue?** Conservatively: motor-vehicle, state aid (highway block grant,
meals & rooms), interest, fees — trended and stress-tested. Over-forecasting revenue is how towns get
caught.

**B14. What's the overlay?** The amount raised above appropriations (RSA 76:6) to cover abatements and
uncollected taxes — a prudent cushion DRA folds into the rate.

**B15. How would you improve financial transparency?** Plain-language budget summaries, a public CIP, a
fund-balance policy in writing, and timely posted financials — trust is built on numbers people can
follow.

## C. Town meeting & SB2 (M5)

**C1. How does SB2 work?** Two sessions under RSA 40:13: deliberative (debate/amend) and voting
(official ballot). Amendments can change amounts/language but can't eliminate an article's subject.

**C2. What is a default budget and when does it apply?** RSA 40:13 IX — last year's appropriations
adjusted for obligations minus one-time costs; if the proposed operating budget fails at the ballot,
the default takes effect. Disclosed at the first budget hearing (40:13 II-a).

**C3. Who calculates the default budget?** The select board, unless the town voted (3/5) to delegate it
to the budget committee.

**C4. Can a default budget be higher than the proposed budget?** Yes — contractual and prior-approved
costs can push it up. I present both numbers side-by-side so no one's ambushed.

**C5. What's a special warrant article?** A non-lapsing/non-transferable, petitioned, capital-reserve,
or specially designated appropriation (RSA 32:3 VI) that sits outside the operating budget with
recommendation tags.

**C6. A citizen petitions an article you think is unwise.** I place it (RSA 39:3 — it's their right),
present it neutrally, and the board may recommend against it. Petitioners are voters; respect comes
first. (Cf. Swanzey's Christian Hill Road bridge petitions.)

**C7. Why does warrant-article wording matter so much?** DRA disallows improperly drafted articles;
"added to" vs. "deposited into" a reserve can void an appropriation. I draft with counsel and the
board like it's contract language.

**C8. The operating budget just failed. Walk us through the next 30 days.** Confirm the default budget
is in effect, communicate service impacts honestly, identify what we lost vs. last year, and start a
listening process to understand the no-vote for next cycle.

**C9. How do you increase voter understanding before the ballot?** Pre-deliberative info nights, a
clear voter guide, accurate recommendation tags, and visible board members. You can't explain at the
ballot box — so you do it before.

**C10. SB2 vs. traditional town meeting — tradeoffs?** SB2 = higher turnout via all-day ballot, but no
floor debate at the decision; traditional = deliberation and decision together, but lower turnout. I
administer either faithfully; the choice is the voters'. (Swanzey rejected repeal 809–345.)

## D. Right-to-Know, records & meetings (M6)

**D1. A resident requests all of the police chief's emails for a month.** Acknowledge within 5 business
days (RSA 91-A), presume openness, analyze exemptions with counsel (personnel/privacy/investigatory),
produce non-exempt records, document the basis for any withholding.

**D2. When can a board go into nonpublic session?** Only for RSA 91-A:3 enumerated reasons (personnel
dismissal/compensation, litigation with counsel, certain real estate). Cite the specific subsection,
roll-call in, keep separate minutes, seal only if statutorily justified.

**D3. Three selectmen are emailing about a contract decision. Problem?** Yes — a deliberating quorum by
email can be an illegal meeting under RSA 91-A. I'd stop it and route the discussion to a noticed
meeting.

**D4. How long must we keep meeting minutes?** Permanently (RSA 33-A); minutes of all public bodies are
permanent records.

**D5. Can we delete old emails to save space?** Not before their retention period expires (RSA 33-A /
91-A); electronic records follow the same schedule as paper.

**D6. What's the notice requirement for a public meeting?** Generally posted 24 hours in advance
(excluding Sundays/holidays) in two public places or the town's posting method.

**D7. How do you handle a hostile, voluminous records requester?** Same law, same professionalism:
acknowledge in time, produce what's public, charge only allowable costs, never retaliate, document
everything. The law doesn't bend for tone.

**D8. How would you systematize Right-to-Know compliance?** A logged intake with the 5-day clock, a
standard exemption checklist, counsel escalation triggers, and a retention-bound archive — make the
deadline a system property, not a memory test.

## E. Personnel & labor (M7)

**E1. A union contract expires mid-year. What governs?** RSA 273-A and the status-quo/"evergreen"
doctrine — terms generally continue; cost items in any new CBA must be funded by the legislative body.

**E2. NHRS employer rates jumped and the board blames the budget on you. Response?** Explain RSA 100-A:
the retirement system sets employer rates; they're non-discretionary. Show the number, its driver, and
options for absorbing it — depersonalize it with facts.

**E3. How do you handle a underperforming department head?** Contemporaneous documentation, clear
expectations, progressive discipline, counsel involvement before termination, and respect for
confidentiality and process.

**E4. Is a personnel file public?** Generally confidential, but certain settlements/discipline can be
public; I'd thread it with counsel under RSA 91-A.

**E5. How do you retain staff in a tight labor market?** Compensation studies, career development,
culture, and realistic workloads — and a written, fairly applied personnel policy so people trust the
rules.

**E6. STAR: a time you managed a difficult personnel situation.** *Situation/Task:* the issue and
stakes. *Action:* documentation, due process, counsel, dignity. *Result:* resolved without litigation,
policy improved. (Bring your own real story.)

## F. Operations, land use, welfare, emergency (M8–M10)

**F1. There's no state procurement statute for towns — so how do you control purchasing?** Install a
written local purchasing policy: bid thresholds, RFP discipline, conflict rules, sole-source
documentation. It's one of the highest-leverage things a new TA can do.

**F2. What's your obligation under RSA 165?** Every town must provide general assistance to residents
unable to support themselves; I'd ensure written guidelines, fair administration, and (often) serve as
or supervise the welfare administrator.

**F3. The river floods and damages two roads. First moves?** Activate the EOP/EOC (RSA 21-P), ensure
public safety, document everything for FEMA public assistance, coordinate mutual aid, and keep the
board and public updated on a known cadence.

**F4. Road classifications — why do they matter?** RSA 231 Class I–VI determines maintenance
responsibility, eligibility for state aid, and what you can/can't be compelled to maintain (e.g.,
Class VI).

**F5. How would you approach economic development on a struggling corridor?** Master plan alignment,
then tools: 79-E community revitalization tax relief, possibly a TIF (RSA 162-K), infrastructure via
CIP, and partnership with the EDA committee. (Cf. Swanzey's Lower Winchester Street project.)

**F6. An aging water system needs evaluation and likely investment. Approach?** Engineering assessment
first, asset-management plan, rate study if it's an enterprise system, capital reserve, and grant/SRF
funding research — sequence it so ratepayers and taxpayers see the plan. (Cf. West Swanzey water.)

## G. Leadership, judgment & behavioral (M12–M14)

**G1. Tell us about a time you delivered hard news to elected officials.** STAR; demonstrate "no
surprises," options-not-problems, and composure under political heat.

**G2. Describe a decision you got wrong and what you learned.** Pick a real one; show ownership,
correction, and a system change so it can't recur. Boards trust people who can say "I was wrong."

**G3. How do you build trust with a board that's been burned by a prior administrator?** Radical
transparency, early small wins, equal treatment of all members, and a written evaluation process so
they can see how I'm doing.

**G4. Two board members can't stand each other. How do you function?** Serve the body, not the feud;
identical information to both; keep every interaction on-record and professional; never become a
faction's instrument.

**G5. How do you handle the press in a controversy?** Be the reliable on-record source: acknowledge
fast, say what I know and don't, name the next step and update time, never speculate. (Keene Sentinel /
My Keene Now market.)

**G6. What's your leadership style?** Servant-leader / force-multiplier: develop department heads,
build systems, push authority and credit down, absorb blame up. Cite ICMA practices 4, 6, 8.

**G7. How do you prioritize when everything's urgent?** Statutory deadlines first (they're
non-negotiable), then risk-weighted impact, then board priorities; communicate the triage so nothing's
a surprise.

**G8. Where are you weakest, and what are you doing about it?** Pick a real growth area mapped to an
ICMA practice and name the concrete development step. Self-awareness reads as strength.

**G9. Why do you want to leave your current role / enter this one?** Forward-looking and positive:
scope, fit, community — never bitterness about a current employer.

**G10. Why THIS town?** Specifics from the town report and minutes: the five villages, SB2 dynamics,
the public-works CIP need, the bridge petitions, the water evaluation, the ambulance-contract cost
pressure. Show you did the homework.

## H. The curveballs (judgment under pressure)

**H1. A board member asks you to "lose" an unfavorable email before a Right-to-Know response.** I
decline, explain it would violate RSA 91-A and RSA 33-A and expose the town and the member to
liability, document the request, and involve counsel. The law and my integrity aren't negotiable —
politely, but immovably.

**H2. You discover your predecessor misallocated grant funds.** Preserve records, notify the board and
counsel/auditor immediately, self-report to the funder if required, and fix the controls. Transparency
now beats exposure later.

**H3. The board votes to do something you believe is unlawful.** Advise clearly (ideally with counsel)
on the record before the vote; if they proceed, document my advice. I implement lawful directives;
I do not implement unlawful ones, and I'd put my objection in writing.

**H4. A popular department head is also the source of a hostile-workplace complaint.** Popularity is
irrelevant; investigate fairly with counsel, protect the complainant from retaliation, follow policy to
the facts wherever they lead.

**H5. It's the night before the deliberative session and you find a warrant-article error.** Assess
with counsel and the moderator whether it can be amended on the floor or must be corrected/withdrawn;
communicate proactively; never let a known defect ride to a vote DRA will disallow.

**H6. Year one, the budget fails and a key staffer quits the same week.** Stabilize: confirm the
default budget, reassure staff and reassign critical duties, communicate calmly to the public and
board, and treat both as signals to diagnose — not panic. Composure is the deliverable.
