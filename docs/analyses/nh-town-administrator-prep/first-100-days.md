# The First 100 Days — A Town Administrator Onboarding Plan

The fastest way to fail is to arrive with answers. The fastest way to succeed is to arrive with
**questions, a notebook, and restraint.** This plan is built on one principle: **earn the authority to
change things by first proving you understand them.** (Mirrors the WORKBOOK Module 15 summary, in
operational detail.)

> Adapt to your town's calendar. If you start in the fall, the tax-rate-setting season collapses your
> "listen" window — front-load the DRA/finance relationships.

---

## Days 1–30 — LISTEN (change nothing)

### People (1:1s — schedule all of these in week one)
- [ ] Each **select-board member**, individually. Ask: "What does success look like in one year? What
      went wrong with the last administrator? What can I never let surprise you?"
- [ ] Every **department head** — at their site, not your office. Ask what they need, what's broken,
      what they're proud of.
- [ ] **Town Clerk and Tax Collector** (likely elected — peers, not reports). Build this bridge early.
- [ ] **Finance/bookkeeper** and the **Trustees of Trust Funds**.
- [ ] **Town counsel** — introduce yourself; understand the open legal matters.
- [ ] **DRA municipal contact** — put a face to the office that sets your rate.
- [ ] **Board/committee chairs** (Planning, ZBA, Conservation, Budget, CIP, etc.).
- [ ] **NHMA / MMANH** — join, register for the next Local Officials Workshop, get *Knowing the
      Territory*.

### Documents (read all of these in month one)
- [ ] Last **3 years of select-board minutes** (public and, as appropriate, nonpublic logs).
- [ ] The most recent **audit / management letter** — your fastest map to financial risk.
- [ ] The current **budget**, **default budget**, and the warrant from the last meeting.
- [ ] The **CIP** and the **capital reserve** balances.
- [ ] Every **collective bargaining agreement** and the **personnel policy**.
- [ ] The **purchasing/procurement policy** (or note its absence — a common early win).
- [ ] **Right-to-Know** request log and the **records-retention** schedule/posture.
- [ ] The **master plan**, zoning ordinance, and any open land-use or economic-development projects.
- [ ] Insurance/risk (Primex) coverage and the open-claims/litigation register.
- [ ] The **emergency operations plan**.

### Posture
- Say "tell me more" far more than "here's what I'd do."
- Attend everything; speak little; take visible notes.
- **Make zero substantive changes.** Fix nothing yet — diagnose.

---

## Days 31–60 — MAP (document the real system)

- [ ] Build the **org chart of authority**: who's elected vs. appointed; who actually decides what.
- [ ] Build the **budget calendar** for the year ahead with every DRA/statutory deadline (see
      `annual-cycle.md`).
- [ ] Build the **open-projects register**: capital projects, grants, litigation, deadlines, risks,
      owners. (Swanzey examples: public-works facility, Christian Hill Road bridge, Lower Winchester
      Street, West Swanzey water, Town Hall ADA access.)
- [ ] Assess the **records / Right-to-Know** posture against RSA 91-A (5-day clock) and RSA 33-A
      (retention) — is there a system, or is it memory-based?
- [ ] Run an honest **ICMA 14-practice self-assessment** and a **town capability** assessment; find the
      two biggest gaps in each.
- [ ] Confirm your own **evaluation framework** with the board — written goals, criteria, and timing.
- [ ] Identify the **single highest-leverage early win** (often: a written purchasing policy, a logged
      Right-to-Know intake, or a clean budget-build template).

---

## Days 61–100 — MOVE (small, visible, durable)

- [ ] Implement that **one** high-leverage system — well, completely, with the board's blessing.
- [ ] Stand up a **standard budget-build process** with department heads for the coming cycle.
- [ ] Deliver a **90-day findings memo** to the board:
      1. what I learned (strengths and risks, candidly),
      2. the year-one priorities (3–5, sequenced),
      3. what I need from the board to deliver them,
      4. how I'd like to be evaluated against them.
- [ ] Establish your **communication cadence**: a weekly board update, a predictable office presence,
      and a "no surprises" habit that's now visible to all three members.
- [ ] Begin the **next budget cycle** on schedule — your first real test, and the board's first real
      read on you.

---

## The four traps to avoid in year one

1. **Acting like a manager when you're an administrator** — overstepping the board's authority. (M1)
2. **Becoming one member's instrument** — favoring the selectman who calls most. (M13)
3. **Skipping the listening tour** — "fixing" things you don't yet understand and stepping on a
   political landmine you couldn't see.
4. **Missing a statutory deadline** — MS-1, MS-232, the budget hearing, the warrant posting. These
   don't forgive a new administrator; the rate can't be set on a missing form.

---

## The one-sentence philosophy

**Spend your first 100 days making the board's authority more effective and the town's systems more
reliable — and the authority to lead will be handed to you, not seized.**
