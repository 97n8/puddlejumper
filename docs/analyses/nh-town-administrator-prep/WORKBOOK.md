# The Town Administrator Preparation Workbook — New Hampshire

**Worked case:** Town of Swanzey, Cheshire County (SB2, 3-member select board, ~7,270 residents,
$8.94M FY2026 operating budget, $20.18 total tax rate). Use Swanzey to make every abstraction
concrete.

# PART I — THE ROLE

## Module 1 — What a New Hampshire Town Administrator Actually Is

The single most important thing to understand before you walk into the interview: **in New Hampshire,
"Town Administrator" is not a statutory office. "Town Manager" is.** Getting this distinction right is
the difference between a candidate who *read about* the job and one who *understands* it.

### 1.1 The three models of NH executive authority

| Model | Statutory basis | Who holds executive authority | Removal |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Town Manager** | RSA 37 `[RSA]` | The *manager* — "administrative head of all departments" (RSA 37:5), with statutory powers delegated by the town's adoption of RSA 37 | At will, for cause, majority of selectmen (RSA 37:2) |
| **Town Administrator** | *No specific statute* — created by the select board under its RSA 41 authority `[RSA]` | The **select board** retains *all* statutory authority; the administrator acts only by delegation | At will of the board; terms set by employment contract |
| **Selectmen-run (no professional)** | RSA 41:8, RSA 41:9 `[RSA]` | The board directly | — |

**The trap question:** *"What's the difference between a town administrator and a town manager?"*

**The strong answer:** "A town manager is a statutory office under RSA 37 — once a town adopts it, the
manager becomes the administrative head of all departments with powers the statute *grants directly*,
subject to selectmen direction but holding real delegated authority over personnel, purchasing, and
day-to-day operations. A town administrator has **no independent statutory authority**. The board of
selectmen remains the governing body under RSA 41:8 — they 'manage the prudential affairs of the
town' — and the administrator exercises only the authority the board chooses to delegate, usually
through a job description, employment agreement, and board policy. Practically the jobs look similar;
legally, a manager has a floor of authority the statute protects, and an administrator's authority is
exactly as wide or narrow as the board's trust. Swanzey runs the **administrator** model `[CASE]`, so
the board is my principal, and my job is to make their authority effective, not to substitute mine for
theirs."

### 1.2 What RSA 37 grants a *manager* (and therefore the ceiling of what a board will delegate)

A town manager has general supervision of property and business affairs and of the expenditure of
appropriated funds, **but that authority does NOT extend to** `[RSA]`:

- warning (calling) town meetings,
- making bylaws,
- borrowing money,
- assessing or collecting taxes.

Specific manager charge areas (RSA 37:6): water/light/power systems; construction, maintenance, and
repair of town buildings and roads; purchase of supplies; police and fire; sewers and drainage; street
lighting; snow removal. **Memorize this list** — it is the practical scope of "running the town," and
even in an administrator town the board typically delegates most of it.

### 1.3 The authority map you carry in your head

```
VOTERS  ──(legislative power: budget, bylaws, bonding, officers)──►  TOWN MEETING / SB2 ballot
   │
   ▼
SELECT BOARD (3, elected)  ──(governing body; RSA 41:8 "prudential affairs")
   │   • sets policy, signs manifests (warrants for $), appoints, hears appeals
   │   • the ONLY body that can bind the town between meetings
   ▼
TOWN ADMINISTRATOR  ──(delegated, at-will, contract)
   │   • runs operations, prepares the budget, supervises staff, executes board policy
   │   • SPEAKS to the board with one voice; does NOT make legislative or money-binding decisions alone
   ▼
DEPARTMENT HEADS  ──(clerk/tax collector are independently *elected* in many towns — a key nuance)
```

**The elected-officer nuance:** In many NH towns the **Town Clerk** and **Tax Collector** are
*elected* (RSA 41) `[RSA]`, not appointed — they do **not** report to you, even though you coordinate
with them daily. A TA who tries to "supervise" an elected clerk creates a political crisis. Know which
of your officials are elected vs. appointed before day one. (Swanzey lists a combined Town
Clerk/Tax Collector function `[CASE]` — confirm its elected/appointed status in the town report.)

### 1.4 The mental model: *you are a force multiplier, not a sovereign*

The best NH administrators describe the job as: **"I make the board's authority effective and the
town's services reliable."** You have enormous *influence* and almost no independent *authority*.
Your power is information, preparation, continuity, and trust. Internalize this and you will never
overstep; miss it and you will be gone within a budget cycle.

## Module 2 — The Governance Ecosystem

You sit at the center of a web of bodies, each with its own statutory lane. Know every lane.

| Body | Authority | Your relationship |
|---|---|---|
| **Town Meeting / SB2 voters** | Legislative — sole power to appropriate, bond, adopt bylaws, elect officers `[RSA 39, 40]` | You inform; you never substitute your judgment for theirs |
| **Select Board** | Governing body; executive between meetings `[RSA 41:8]` | Your direct principal; one voice, three votes |
| **Budget Committee** (if adopted) | Recommends budget; under RSA 32:14–24 prepares it on MS-737 `[RSA]` | You staff it with data; it is independent of the board |
| **Moderator** | Presides over meeting/deliberative session; rules on procedure `[RSA 40:4]` | Coordinate warrant + process; respect their control of the floor |
| **Planning Board / ZBA / Conservation** | Land use, variances, natural resources `[RSA 673–677]` | Staff support; quasi-judicial — never lobby them privately |
| **Trustees of Trust Funds** | Custody of capital reserve & trust funds `[RSA 31:22, 35]` | Coordinate appropriations into/out of reserves |
| **DRA — Municipal & Property Division** | Sets the tax rate; reviews forms `[DRA]` | Your fall counterpart; miss their deadlines and the rate can't be set |
| **NHMA** (NH Municipal Association) | Training, legal hotline, *Knowing the Territory* `[NHMA]` | Your first call on any legal-gray question |
| **MMANH** (Municipal Management Assoc. of NH) | Your professional peer body; annual conf each June `[NHMA]` | Join it — your network is your early-warning system |

**Swanzey's standing boards** `[CASE]`: Planning Board, Zoning Board of Adjustment, Conservation
Commission, Energy Commission, Capital Improvement Program Committee, Economic Development Advisory
Committee, Rail Trails Advisory Committee, Trustees of Trust Funds, and a Swanzey Governance Committee.
Nine volunteer bodies for one staff to support — a realistic load, and a reason the job is about
*systems*, not heroics.

# PART II — THE CRAFT (domains of mastery)

## Module 3 — Municipal Finance & Budgeting (the core competency)

If you master one domain, make it this one. In NH, the budget *is* the policy.

### 3.1 The Municipal Budget Law — RSA 32 `[RSA]`

The governing statute. The concepts that matter:

- **Appropriation by purpose (RSA 32:3).** Money is appropriated for *purposes*, not by your wish.
  The "operating budget" is the bottom line for general operations; **special warrant articles** and
  **individual warrant articles** sit outside it.
- **Limitation on expenditures (RSA 32:8).** No board, agency, or official may pay or agree to pay
  more than the amount appropriated for that purpose. This is the legal teeth.
- **Transfers between appropriations (RSA 32:10).** Unless the meeting voted line-item, the governing
  body *may* transfer between purposes within the total — a critical management flexibility you must
  understand and document.
- **Lapse (RSA 32:7).** Appropriations lapse at fiscal year-end unless properly encumbered or made
  non-lapsing by vote. You cannot "save up" an operating line for next year by default.
- **Emergency over-expenditure (RSA 32:11).** You may exceed an appropriation only via the emergency
  process — which requires **DRA approval**. Know it exists; pray you never need it.

### 3.2 The forms: MS-636 vs MS-737 `[DRA]`

| Form | Used by | Purpose |
|---|---|---|
| **MS-636** | Towns *without* an official budget committee (have not adopted RSA 32:14–24) | The proposed budget |
| **MS-737** | Towns *with* an official budget committee | Budget prepared by the committee; **certified by a majority of the committee and submitted to DRA within 20 days** of the meeting |
| **MS-DT / MS-DTB** | SB2 towns | The **default budget** worksheet |
| **MS-232** | All municipalities | "Appropriations as Voted" — filed **within 20 days** after the meeting |
| **MS-1** | All | Summary of valuation — **due September 1** |
| **MS-535** | All | Year-end financial report |
| **MS-434** | All | Revised estimated revenues |

The budget must be prepared per RSA 32:5 and **posted with the warrant** under RSA 31:95 `[RSA]`.

### 3.3 Fund balance — the lever every board asks about

**Unassigned fund balance (UFB)** is the town's accumulated surplus. DRA guidance is to retain
roughly **5%–17%** of the total operating budget (often cited as a prudent target near the upper
single digits to low teens) as a hedge for cash flow and emergencies. Each year the board decides how
much UFB to **"apply to reduce the tax rate."** This is the single most powerful — and most
politically loaded — number you manage.

- Apply *too much* → lower taxes this year, but you erode the cushion and spike the rate next year.
- Apply *too little* → defensible reserves, but a board facing reelection will push back hard.

**Interview gold:** "I treat fund balance as a multi-year smoothing tool, not a one-year tax cut. I'd
bring the board a three-year UFB trajectory so the decision is made with eyes open, and I'd document
the bond-rating and cash-flow rationale for whatever target we set." For Swanzey, on an ~$8.94M
operating budget `[CASE]`, a 10% target is roughly **$0.9M** — name that number and you sound like
you've done it.

### 3.4 Capital planning — CIP & reserves `[RSA]`

- **Capital Improvement Program (RSA 674:5–8):** a 6-year (minimum) rolling plan of capital projects;
  in many towns a CIP Committee builds it (Swanzey has one `[CASE]`). The CIP is how you stop "surprise
  capital" from blowing up a tax rate.
- **Capital Reserve Funds (RSA 35):** voters appropriate into a reserve held by the **Trustees of
  Trust Funds** (RSA 31:22); spent later for the stated purpose — the disciplined way to save for a
  fire truck or a public-works facility.
- **Expendable trust funds (RSA 31:19-a):** flexible reserves for specified purposes.

**Swanzey worked example** `[CASE]`: the **$1.2M public-works facility** failed its 3/5 supermajority
in 2026 (681–485 on recount). A strong TA response: "A capital ask that big shouldn't be a single
up-or-down bond vote cold. I'd build it into the CIP, pre-fund a capital reserve over 3–4 years to
shrink the bond, hold the public information sessions *before* the deliberative session, and bring it
back as a smaller, better-understood number. The 3/5 threshold under RSA 33 for bonding `[RSA]` means
you win these on preparation, not persuasion at the ballot."

### 3.5 Borrowing — RSA 33, the Municipal Finance Act `[RSA]`

Long-term debt (bonds/notes) generally requires a **3/5 supermajority** of the meeting. You don't
issue debt; you *prepare the case* and manage the bond counsel/financial-advisor relationship. Know
debt-service-to-budget ratios and what your bond rating turns on (fund balance, debt load, management
practices — the rating agencies literally score whether the town has a professional administrator).

## Module 4 — The NH Annual Cycle & Tax Rate Setting

This is the metronome of the job. A TA who can recite the year by heart is instantly credible.

### 4.1 The tax-rate-setting sequence `[DRA]`

NH towns don't set their own rate — **DRA sets it**, by combining four components:
**municipal + county + state education (SWEPT) + local education**. (Swanzey FY2025: $5.62 + $2.28 +
$1.25 + $11.03 = **$20.18** `[CASE]`.) Education is ~61% of the bill and outside your control — say so,
so a board never thinks you can wave away the school number.

The DRA process:

1. **By Sept 1** — file **MS-1** (summary of valuation), plus **MS-434** (revised revenues) and
   **MS-535** (financial report).
2. **After town meeting (+20 days)** — file **MS-232** "Appropriations as Voted," with the meeting
   minutes; DRA verifies warrant-article language against the minutes and will **disallow** improperly
   drafted articles. *(This is why warrant-article drafting precision matters — see Module 5.)*
3. **Fall** — DRA reviews, applies the **equalization ratio** (RSA 21-J:3 `[RSA]`) for county/state
   apportionment, accounts for the **overlay** (RSA 76:6 — a cushion raised for abatements/uncollected
   taxes) and **war-service/elderly/disabled credits and exemptions** (RSA 72), and **sets the rate**.
4. **Tax bills issue** (semi-annual billing under RSA 76:15-a in most towns).

### 4.2 The administrator's year (condensed — full version in `annual-cycle.md`)

- **Summer/Fall:** close prior year, file MS-1/MS-535, work the tax rate with DRA, issue fall tax bill,
  begin next-year budget build with department heads.
- **Oct–Dec:** budget development; CIP updates; **public hearing(s) on the budget** (RSA 32:5,
  RSA 40:13 II-a for SB2); finalize **default budget** (SB2).
- **Jan–Feb:** post warrant + budget (RSA 31:95); **deliberative session** (SB2 first session,
  late Jan–early Feb; Swanzey: first Tuesday in February `[CASE]`) — voters debate and amend.
- **March:** **voting day** (SB2 second session; Swanzey: second Tuesday in March `[CASE]`); officer
  elections; warrant articles decided by official ballot.
- **Mar–Apr:** file MS-232 within 20 days; implement what passed; issue spring tax bill; set the new
  year in motion.

## Module 5 — Town Meeting & SB2 Mastery (RSA 40:13)

In an SB2 town, the warrant *is* the law you'll live under for a year. Precision here separates pros
from amateurs.

### 5.1 SB2 structure `[RSA]`

RSA 40:13 splits the meeting into two sessions:

- **First (deliberative) session:** explanation, debate, and **amendment** of the operating budget and
  warrant articles. Voters can amend dollar amounts and language **but cannot eliminate the subject
  matter** of an article (NHMA/court guidance). Whatever survives goes to the ballot *as amended*.
- **Second (voting) session:** official **ballot** vote — no floor debate. This is why turnout and
  pre-meeting communication matter so much: you can't explain at the ballot box.

### 5.2 The default budget — the concept boards always test you on `[RSA]`

Because SB2 voters can reject the proposed operating budget by ballot, RSA 40:13, IX requires a
**default budget**: essentially last year's appropriations adjusted for contractual obligations and
previously-approved costs, minus one-time items. **If the operating-budget article fails, the default
budget takes effect.**

- The **local governing body (select board)** calculates it — *unless* the town has voted (3/5) to
  delegate that to the budget committee.
- It must be **disclosed at the first budget public hearing** (RSA 40:13, II-a) on the DRA default-
  budget form.

**The killer interview answer:** "The default budget is the floor — and in a contentious year it can
be *higher or lower* than what we proposed, which surprises people. My job is to calculate it honestly
under RSA 40:13 IX, disclose it at the public hearing, and make sure the board understands the
strategic reality: if voters distrust the proposed budget, the default is what they get. So the
default budget isn't a back-pocket trick; it's a transparency obligation, and I'd present both numbers
side-by-side so no one feels ambushed."

### 5.3 Special vs. individual warrant articles `[RSA]`

- **Special warrant articles (RSA 32:3, VI):** appropriations that are non-lapsing/non-transferable,
  or petitioned, or for capital reserves, or designated special — they sit *outside* the operating
  budget and carry recommendation tags from the board (and budget committee).
- **Petitioned articles (RSA 39:3):** citizens can force an article onto the warrant by petition
  (e.g., Swanzey's Christian Hill Road bridge petitions `[CASE]`). You must place them; you may
  recommend against them. Handle them with scrupulous neutrality — petitioners are voters too.

### 5.4 Warrant drafting precision

DRA will **disallow** an article whose language doesn't match what was voted, or that's improperly
constructed `[DRA]`. The TA typically drafts the warrant with the board and town counsel. One sloppy
"shall be deposited into a capital reserve fund" vs. "to be added to" can cost the town the
appropriation. Treat warrant language like contract language, because it is.

## Module 6 — Right-to-Know & Records (RSA 91-A / RSA 33-A)

The fastest way to a lawsuit and a front-page story is a botched Right-to-Know response. Own this.

### 6.1 RSA 91-A — the Right-to-Know Law `[RSA]`

- **Presumption of openness:** records and meetings are public unless the body *proves* an exemption.
- **Response within 5 business days:** you must produce, deny with a statutory reason, or state when
  the records will be available.
- **Electronic records** are public and retained the same as paper; you **cannot delete** a record
  before its retention period expires.
- **Meetings:** public bodies must post notice (generally 24 hours, excluding Sundays/holidays); a
  quorum deliberating town business = a meeting, **including email chains** — never "reply-all" a
  decision among a board quorum.

### 6.2 Nonpublic sessions — RSA 91-A:3 `[RSA]`

You may enter nonpublic session **only** for enumerated reasons (the big ones: personnel —
"the dismissal, promotion, or compensation of any public employee"; certain real-estate acquisition;
consultation with counsel on litigation; etc.). You must: cite the **specific subsection** in the
motion, roll-call in, keep separate minutes, and **seal** minutes only if disclosure would render the
action ineffective or affect reputation. A TA who can recite the entrance procedure cold is worth
their salary the first time a board tries to slide into nonpublic for the wrong reason.

### 6.3 Records retention — RSA 33-A `[RSA]`

RSA 33-A:3-a sets minimum retention for **156 categories**. Anchors: town-meeting and public-body
**minutes are permanent**; property inventories and abatement records **5 years**; time cards
**4 years**; transitory correspondence as-needed. A record may not be legally destroyed before its
clock runs (this dovetails with RSA 91-A). (Full mapping lives in the companion Swanzey VAULT
retention map.)

**Why a governance engine matters here:** missing a 5-day clock or deleting a record early is a
*systemic* failure, not a personal one — exactly the failure class a fail-closed records workflow is
designed to make impossible. (This is the bridge to your PublicLogic/VAULT thesis: make the statutory
clock and the retention class machine-enforced, not memory-dependent.)

## Module 7 — Personnel & Labor

People are 60–75% of a municipal budget. Mishandle personnel and nothing else matters.

- **PELRB / collective bargaining — RSA 273-A `[RSA]`:** if any unit is organized (often police, fire,
  public works), you negotiate within RSA 273-A. **Cost items in a CBA must be funded by the
  legislative body** — you can agree, but voters fund. Know the "evergreen"/status-quo doctrine: terms
  often continue after a contract expires.
- **NH Retirement System — RSA 100-A `[RSA]`:** Group I (employees/teachers) and Group II (police/
  fire). Employer rates are set by the system and are a major, non-discretionary budget driver — be
  ready to explain a rate increase to a board that wants to blame you for it.
- **Right-to-Know & personnel:** personnel files are generally confidential, but *settlements and
  certain discipline* can be public — thread this carefully with counsel.
- **At-will vs. for-cause; progressive discipline; documentation:** the unglamorous core. The TA who
  documents performance contemporaneously wins the grievance and the unemployment hearing.
- **Your own contract:** you are at-will (administrator model). Negotiate severance, notice, and a
  clear evaluation process up front — the ICMA model employment agreement is your template `[ICMA]`.

## Module 8 — Public Works, Roads & Capital Assets

- **Highways — RSA 231 `[RSA]`:** classification (Class I–VI), layout/discontinuance, **scenic roads**
  (RSA 231:157), driveway permits. Most of your capital and a lot of your politics live in the road
  budget.
- **Procurement:** NH has **no single state procurement statute for towns** — you operate under *local*
  purchasing policy. That means *you* (with the board) set the bid thresholds, the RFP discipline, and
  the conflict rules. A clean, written purchasing policy is one of the highest-leverage things a new TA
  can install.
- **Solid waste — RSA 149-M `[RSA]`:** transfer stations/recycling (Swanzey runs a recycling center
  `[CASE]`); know your district/host obligations.
- **Capital asset stewardship:** facilities, fleet, GASB 34 capital reporting. Deferred maintenance is
  a tax increase you haven't told anyone about yet — surface it in the CIP.

## Module 9 — Land Use & Economic Development

- **Planning & zoning — RSA 672–677 `[RSA]`:** master plan (RSA 674:1), zoning ordinance (674:16),
  subdivision/site-plan review (674:35–44), the **CIP (674:5–8)**. Land-use boards are
  *quasi-judicial* — you support them with staff and counsel but **never** lobby them.
- **Local land use boards — RSA 673 `[RSA]`:** composition, alternates, conflicts.
- **Economic development tools:** **TIF districts — RSA 162-K**; **Community Revitalization Tax Relief
  (79-E) `[RSA]`**; corridor/village revitalization. Swanzey's Economic Development Advisory Committee
  and Lower Winchester Street project `[CASE]` are exactly where these tools apply.
- **The growth-vs-character tension:** the perennial NH small-town politics. Your job is to give boards
  *options with consequences*, not to pick the town's identity for it.

## Module 10 — Welfare, Emergency Management & Public Safety

- **Local welfare / general assistance — RSA 165 `[RSA]`:** every NH town **must** provide assistance
  to residents unable to support themselves. The TA is frequently the **Welfare Administrator** or
  supervises one. Guidelines must be written and adopted; assistance is a statutory duty, not
  discretion. NHMA's *Art of Welfare Administration* is the manual `[NHMA]`.
- **Emergency management — RSA 21-P `[RSA]`:** local emergency operations plan, the EOC, NIMS, and the
  FEMA public-assistance process after a declared disaster. In a flood-prone Ashuelot River town
  `[CASE]`, this is not theoretical.
- **Public safety:** police (RSA 105), fire (RSA 154; Swanzey runs **three fire stations** `[CASE]`),
  the ambulance/EMS contract (a Swanzey FY2026 cost-driver `[CASE]`). You manage the budget and the
  mutual-aid relationships; you do **not** run operations — respect the chief's command authority.

## Module 11 — Risk, Legal, Ethics & Liability

- **Ethics & conflicts — RSA 31:39-a `[RSA]`:** towns may adopt a code of ethics; conflict-of-interest
  recusal is non-negotiable. The appearance standard matters as much as the actual conflict.
- **Official immunity & indemnification — RSA 31:104–105 `[RSA]`:** the town may indemnify officials
  acting within scope; know the limits.
- **Insurance/risk pools:** most NH towns use **Primex** or NHMA's pools for property/liability and
  workers' comp (RSA 281-A). Risk management is a budget line *and* a culture.
- **When to call counsel / NHMA:** the mark of a mature TA is knowing the edge of their competence. The
  NHMA legal hotline and town counsel are not signs of weakness — failing to use them is.

## Module 13 — The Select-Board Relationship (the #1 predictor of survival)

Every TA who fails, fails *here*, not on a budget formula. The rules:

1. **No surprises.** A board member should never learn town news from a constituent or the *Keene
   Sentinel* `[CASE]` first. Brief early, brief everyone, brief equally.
2. **One body, not three bosses.** You serve the board's *majority vote*, not the individual who calls
   most. Treat all three members identically — same information, same access.
3. **Policy is theirs; administration is yours.** When a member tries to direct an employee or a vendor
   directly, redirect to the board-as-a-whole, gently and consistently.
4. **Bring options, not problems.** "Here are three paths, the cost and risk of each, and my
   recommendation" — every time.
5. **Manage your own evaluation.** Insist on a written annual evaluation against agreed goals (ICMA
   handbook model `[ICMA]`). Ambiguity in how you're judged is how good TAs get blindsided.

## Module 14 — Communication, Public Meetings & Crisis

- **Running a public meeting:** agenda discipline, consent agendas, RSA 91-A notice, minutes that are
  accurate-not-verbatim, and a moderator/chair you've prepped.
- **The press:** be the reliable, on-record source. In Swanzey's media market (Keene Sentinel, My
  Keene Now `[CASE]`), a TA who returns calls and tells the truth controls the narrative; one who hides
  loses it.
- **Crisis playbook:** flood, water-system failure (Swanzey's West Swanzey water evaluation `[CASE]`),
  a personnel scandal, a failed budget. The pattern is always: *acknowledge fast, say what you know and
  don't, name the next step and when you'll update, never speculate.*

## Module 15 — Interview Mastery & First 100 Days

### 15.1 How NH TA interviews actually run

Usually a **select-board panel** (sometimes plus a search consultant), often a public "meet the
finalists" component, and frequently a **writing or in-basket exercise** (draft a memo, critique a
warrant article, build a mini-budget scenario). They are hiring for **judgment, temperament, and
trust** at least as much as technical knowledge — because the technical knowledge they can verify, but
they have to *live with* your judgment for years.

### 15.2 The seven answers you must have ready

1. **TA vs. Manager / "what authority do you think you have here?"** → Module 1. Show you understand
   you serve the board.
2. **"Walk us through how you'd build next year's budget."** → Module 3 + 4: department asks → CIP →
   default budget → public hearing → warrant → DRA. Name the forms.
3. **"A resident files a Right-to-Know request for the chief's emails. What do you do?"** → Module 6:
   5 business days, presumption of openness, exemption analysis with counsel, document everything.
4. **"A select-board member tells a DPW employee to plow their road first. What do you do?"** →
   Module 13: redirect to the board-as-a-whole, protect the chain, no public confrontation.
5. **"The operating budget just failed at the ballot. Now what?"** → Module 5: default budget takes
   effect under RSA 40:13 IX; here's how I'd manage services and rebuild trust for next year.
6. **"Tell us about a time you delivered hard news to elected officials."** → STAR story; show "no
   surprises" and options-not-problems.
7. **"Why this town?"** → Specifics. For Swanzey: the five villages, the SB2 dynamics, the public-works
   facility that needs a CIP runway, the bridge petitions, the water-system evaluation `[CASE]`. Show
   you read the town report and the last year of meeting minutes.

### 15.3 Your first 100 days (summary — full plan in `first-100-days.md`)

- **Days 1–30 — Listen:** meet every department head, every board chair, the DRA contact, town counsel,
  and each selectman 1:1. Read three years of minutes, the last audit, the CIP, every CBA, and the
  personnel policy. **Change nothing.**
- **Days 31–60 — Map:** document the budget calendar, the org chart (who's elected vs. appointed),
  the open capital projects, the litigation/risk register, and the records/retention posture.
- **Days 61–100 — Move (small):** install or refresh the *one* highest-leverage system (often a written
  purchasing policy, a Right-to-Know intake process, or a clean budget-build template), and bring the
  board a 90-day findings memo with a prioritized year-one plan.
