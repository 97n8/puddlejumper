# The Swanzey Capital Forecast Book

### How to fund every capital need the town has — without another failed bond vote

**Subject:** Town of Swanzey, NH · **Horizon:** FY2027–FY2036 (10 years) · **Prepared:** 2026-06
**Methodology:** NH municipal capital finance (RSA 35 reserves, RSA 33/35-A borrowing, RSA 234 bridge
aid, NHDES DWSRF, block grants, CDBG) sequenced through a RSA 674:5–8 Capital Improvement Program.

> **Provenance.** `[R]` real/sourced · `[D]` derived · `[M]` modeled (planning estimate). Project
> *costs* are `[M]` planning estimates pending engineering; *funding mechanics* and statutory shares
> are `[R]`. Valuation and rate math use the lab's Swanzey-calibrated base (total assessed value
> ≈ $978.6M; municipal rate $5.62; $100k of tax-funded capital ≈ $0.10 on the rate). Replace `[M]`
> figures with the town's real numbers before adopting. This book proves the **strategy**; the town's
> engineers and DRA confirm the **digits.**

## 0. The thesis (read this if you read nothing else)

Swanzey's capital problem looks like five separate worries — a failed public-works facility, a
contested historic bridge, a street, a water system, an ADA project. It is actually **one** problem,
and it has **one** solution:

> **You cannot win a $1.2M 3/5 bond vote with a better pamphlet. You win by restructuring the money
> so the scary vote disappears.**

Do three things and every worry is solved:

1. **Make other people pay for most of it.** Bridge aid covers **80%** of the bridge (RSA 234) `[R]`.
   The water system runs through the **DWSRF** — a 1% loan repaid by water *rates*, plus grant-funded
   engineering and possible **principal forgiveness** `[R]` — so it leaves the property tax almost
   untouched. Block grants and CDBG carry the street and the ADA project. **62% of the backlog is
   non-property-tax money.** `[D]`
2. **Convert the local remainder from a bond into reserves.** A bond needs a **3/5 supermajority** in
   an SB2 town (RSA 33) `[R]` — the exact wall the $1.2M facility hit twice (681–485) `[R]`. A
   **capital reserve fund** (RSA 35) needs only a **simple majority** to fund `[R]`. Save up, pay cash,
   skip the supermajority entirely.
3. **Smooth it so the rate never spikes.** Phase the reserve deposits so the *net-new* capital impact
   peaks at about **+$0.25 on the municipal rate** (~**+$75/yr** on a $300k home `[D]`) for two years,
   then falls **below today's** capital burden once the backlog clears.

**The result:** **~$5.3M of capital needs, fully funded** `[D]`, **38% of it local**, paid through
**majority-vote reserves**, **zero new bonds**, **zero 3/5 votes**, and a property-tax impact you can
print on a postcard. That is what "solving all their capital worries" means.

## 1. Why the old approach failed (and will keep failing)

The 2026 playbook was: identify a need ($1.2M public-works facility), write one bond article, ask the
voters for a 3/5 supermajority, and lose 681–485 `[R]`. Then everything else — bridge, street, water,
ADA — waits behind it, unfunded, aging, getting more expensive.

This fails structurally, not politically, for four reasons:

| Failure mode | Why it happens | The fix in this book |
|---|---|---|
| **The supermajority wall** | One big bond needs 3/5; 55% "yes" still loses | Reserves need only a majority (RSA 35) |
| **Sticker shock** | $1.2M as a single number reads as a tax bomb | $200k–$300k annual deposits read as prudent saving |
| **No leverage** | Paying 100% local when the state would pay 80% of a bridge | Stack aid/SRF/grants first, local last |
| **No runway** | Cold-voting a project voters haven't seen | A published CIP makes the ask the *expected* next step |

A failed 3/5 vote isn't a messaging problem. It's a **financing-structure** problem. Change the
structure and the politics change with it.

## 3. The funding stack — make other people pay first

The core move. For every project, exhaust the funding ladder **top-down** before a local dollar is
touched. Full detail in [`funding-stack.csv`](./funding-stack.csv).

### The ladder (always in this order)

1. **State/federal categorical aid** — bridge aid, highway block grant (free-er than free: you lose it
   if you don't use it).
2. **Enterprise / revolving-fund financing** — DWSRF/CWSRF for water/sewer, repaid by *rates*, off the
   tax base.
3. **Competitive grants** — CDBG, NHDES asset-management & planning grants, principal forgiveness.
4. **Local capital reserves (RSA 35)** — pay-go savings, majority vote.
5. **Bonding (RSA 33 / Bond Bank RSA 35-A)** — last resort, only for the un-shrinkable remainder.

### Applied to Swanzey

| Project | Total | Non-tax money | Local tax share | Local % | How |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Public Works Facility | $1.20M | $0 | **$1.20M** | 100% | Pay-go reserve (the one fully-local item) |
| Christian Hill Bridge | $1.00M | **$0.80M** | $0.20M | 20% | **RSA 234 bridge aid (80% state)** `[R]` |
| Lower Winchester St | $0.75M | $0.27M | $0.48M | 64% | Highway block grant + roads reserve |
| West Swanzey Water | $2.075M | **$2.075M** | **$0** | 0% | **DWSRF loan (water rates) + $75k AM grant + $400k principal forgiveness** `[R]` |
| Town Hall ADA | $0.25M | $0.125M | $0.125M | 50% | CDBG/accessibility grant + facilities reserve |
| **TOTAL** | **$5.275M** | **$3.27M (62%)** | **$2.005M (38%)** | — | **$0 bonds** |

**The headline, in one sentence:** *Swanzey is staring at $5.3M of capital needs and can retire all of
it for about $2.0M of local money — spread over a decade, raised through majority-vote reserve
deposits, with not a single bond and not a single 3/5 supermajority.* `[D/M]`

The single biggest unlock is the **water system**: route it through the DWSRF/enterprise channel and a
$2.075M problem stops being a property-tax problem at all `[R]`. The second is the **bridge**: the
state pays 80% — the town's job is to fund a $200k share, not a $1M project.

less than today forever, with everything fixed.

Compare that to the status quo: a $0 "savings," a failed bond, a DPW still in an undersized garage, and
a bridge fight with no money behind it. **The smooth, financed path is cheaper *and* calmer than the
do-nothing path** once you count deferred-maintenance inflation.

## 5. The financing playbook (the tools, with the rules that matter)

Reference detail in [`financing-playbook.md`](./financing-playbook.md). The essentials:

### Capital Reserve Funds — RSA 35 `[R]`
- Established and funded by **simple majority** vote (the whole point).
- Held by the **Trustees of Trust Funds** (RSA 31:22); invested per RSA 35:9.
- Designate a purpose (specific or by *type*); a named agent (often the select board) may expend
  without a further meeting vote if so authorized.
- **Strategy:** stand up one reserve per project/asset class now; fund them annually; they *are* your
  CIP's bank.

### State Bridge Aid — RSA 234 `[R]`
- **State pays 80%, town 20%** of eligible municipal bridge construction/reconstruction, subject to
  annual appropriation/queue.
- Eligibility: clear span **≥ 10 ft**; **biennial inspection compliance** is a prerequisite to apply.
- NHDOT notifies towns of **red-list** bridges (NBI condition ≤ 4) by **April 1** annually.
- **Strategy for Christian Hill:** get/keep the inspection current, apply, bank the 20% share in the
  bridge reserve while you sit in the queue. The petitioners' "study it for a year" request and the
  bridge-aid timeline are the *same* runway — use it.

### Drinking Water State Revolving Fund (DWSRF) — NHDES `[R]`
- **1% interest during construction**; repayment begins ~1 year after substantial completion.
- **Principal forgiveness** for disadvantaged systems / where water rates exceed the affordability
  threshold.
- **Asset-management** and **strategic-planning grants** fund the up-front engineering/PER.
- Annual **spring pre-application**; projects ranked on the Intended Use Plan priority list; an
  **Asset Management & Renewal Plan (AMRP)** is required for funded assets.
- **Strategy for West Swanzey:** grant-fund the evaluation now, pre-apply this spring, structure
  repayment through **water rates** (with a rate study), and chase principal forgiveness. Keep it off
  the tax rate.

### Bonding — RSA 33 / NH Municipal Bond Bank RSA 35-A `[R]`
- Long-term debt needs **3/5** in SB2 towns. Use the **Bond Bank** for pooled, low-cost rates if you
  must borrow.
- **Strategy:** in this plan you don't borrow at all. Hold bonding in reserve for a true emergency
  (a bridge that fails inspection, a water main break) — and if you ever need it, a town with funded
  reserves and a published CIP gets a **better rate** and an easier 3/5 vote.

### Highway Block Grant / CDBG / other `[R]`
- Block grant aid (RSA 235) is annual road money — sweep a portion into the roads reserve for Lower
  Winchester rather than spreading it thin. CDBG funds accessibility/community projects like the ADA
  stairwell.

## 6. The warrant & communication strategy (how it actually passes)

Financing structure does most of the work; presentation finishes it.

1. **Establish the reserves first, in a low-stakes year.** RSA 35 establishment + a modest first
   deposit is a majority vote and an easy "yes" — it commits the town to *nothing* it can't stop.
2. **Show the CIP every year (RSA 674:5–8).** When the public-works facility deposit is line 4 of a
   published 10-year plan voters have seen three times, it's not a surprise — it's the plan working.
3. **Lead with the leverage.** The warrant article and the voter guide should say the quiet part loud:
   *"This bridge costs $1M; the state pays $800k; we're banking our $200k share."* People vote for
   80%-off.
4. **Pre-deliberative-session information nights.** You cannot explain at the SB2 ballot box (Module 5
   of the workbook) — so explain before. Bring the glide-path table from §4; the "+$75 then −$18" story
   sells itself.
5. **Never bundle.** One reserve, one purpose, one clean article. Bundling is how good projects drag
   each other down.
6. **Name the default-budget reality.** Reserve deposits are *appropriations*; if the operating article
   fails, deposits may not happen. Tie the CIP to budget passage so voters see the linkage.

## 7. Risk register & the answers

| Risk | Mitigation in this plan |
|---|---|
| Bridge aid is queued/underfunded some years | Bank the 20% share anyway; the reserve waits patiently and the town is "shovel-ready" when aid comes |
| DWSRF ranking misses a cycle | Re-apply next spring; the AM grant + evaluation keep the project competitive; interim needs from water rates |
| A reserve deposit fails at the ballot | It's a majority vote, not 3/5 — far likelier to pass; and a miss only slows, never kills, the plan |
| Cost estimates rise (inflation) | Estimates are `[M]`; the CIP is re-priced annually with engineering; reserves flex |
| Valuation shifts at revaluation | Rate impacts re-computed on the new MS-1 base; the *dollar* deposits are what's voted |
| "We can't afford anything" politics | The −$18 endpoint and the 62%-other-people's-money headline are the counter-argument |
| An emergency hits mid-plan | Reserves + a funded-CIP bond rating + RSA 33 bonding as the genuine last resort |

## 8. The one-page board recommendation

> **Recommendation.** Adopt a 10-year Capital Improvement Program built on six capital reserve funds
> (Public Works Facility, Bridges, Roads, Highway Equipment, Fire Apparatus, Facilities) plus a
> technology reserve, funded by simple-majority annual deposits. Pursue **RSA 234 bridge aid (80%)**
> for Christian Hill Road, **NHDES DWSRF financing through water rates** for West Swanzey, and
> **CDBG/block-grant** aid for the ADA project and Lower Winchester Street. Build the public-works
> facility **pay-go** from its reserve by 2031 — no bond, no 3/5 vote. Net effect: the entire
> ~$5.3M capital backlog is funded, 62% of it with non-property-tax money, the peak tax impact is about
> **+$75/year on a $300,000 home for two years**, and after 2031 the town's capital burden is **lower
> than it is today** with every project complete.
>
> **In plain terms: we stop losing bond votes, we start banking the state's money, and we fix
> everything — for the price of a tank of oil a year, briefly, then less than now.**
