## There's a way to fix Swanzey's roads, bridge, and garage — without another failed bond

Twice now, Swanzey voters have been asked to approve a $1.2 million bond for a new public works
facility. Twice it has come up short — not because most voters opposed it, but because a bond requires a
three-fifths supermajority, and 678 "yes" votes out of 1,163 simply wasn't enough. Meanwhile, the
Christian Hill Road bridge, Lower Winchester Street, the West Swanzey water system, and accessibility at
Town Hall all wait in line behind it, getting older and more expensive every year.

Here is the thing we keep getting wrong: a failed bond isn't a sign the town doesn't want the project.
It's a sign we're using the wrong financial tool. And there is a better one.

Start with a number that should change the whole conversation: of the roughly **$5.3 million** in
capital work Swanzey needs over the next decade, only about **$2 million is actually ours to pay.** The
rest — **62 percent** — is money the town is currently leaving on the table.

Take the Christian Hill Road bridge. Under New Hampshire's Municipal Bridge Aid program, the **state
pays 80 percent** of an eligible town bridge. Our job isn't to fund a million-dollar bridge; it's to
save up a $200,000 share and let Concord cover the other $800,000. Or the West Swanzey water system:
water projects qualify for a state revolving-fund loan at **1 percent interest, repaid through water
rates** — by the people who use the system — plus grants and possible loan forgiveness. Done right, that
$2 million project barely touches your property tax bill at all. The Town Hall accessibility work
qualifies for a community grant. Lower Winchester Street can draw on state highway aid.

Once you stack up all that outside money first, the local share shrinks to something manageable. And
here's the second move: instead of borrowing it with a bond that needs a three-fifths vote, we **save
it** in capital reserve funds — town savings accounts, each with a dedicated purpose, that need only a
**simple majority** to create and fund. We set aside money each year, and when the fund is full, we pay
cash. No interest. No supermajority. No bond vote to lose. The public works facility gets built around
2031 — paid for, not borrowed.

What does this cost you? On a $300,000 home, the *added* tax impact peaks at about **$75 a year** — for
a single year — and runs $36 to $66 in the other build years. Then something remarkable happens. Once
the projects are built, the capital portion of your tax bill drops to roughly **$18 below where it is
today.** You pay a little more for about five years, then less than now, with every project finished.
Roughly the cost of one tank of heating oil, briefly, to fix everything.

People reasonably ask: isn't this just more spending dressed up? No. These are repairs the town must
make either way — deferring them only raises the price. This plan actually *lowers* the total cost by
capturing aid we're otherwise forfeiting, and it replaces the boom-and-bust of surprise bond votes with
a steady, published, ten-year plan. The reason the garage became a crisis is that the town never set
aside a dollar for it. This fixes that permanently.

It's also safe. Every deposit is a simple-majority vote you can pause. The bridge money is written to be
contingent on state aid, so if the 80 percent doesn't come, the town isn't on the hook. The water loan
is repaid by ratepayers, not taxpayers. And if voters skip a year's deposit, the plan simply slows — it
doesn't collapse the way a single failed bond does.

None of this asks you to take numbers on faith. The full Capital Forecast Book, with the math behind
every figure and the actual warrant articles, is public, and the costs are confirmed by engineers, the
state, and the Department of Revenue Administration before anything is built.

Swanzey doesn't have a spending problem or a willpower problem. It has a *structure* problem — and
structure is fixable. Let's stop losing bond votes, start banking the state's money, and get this town's
long list of "someday" projects done.

*— [Name], [Town role or "Swanzey resident"]*
