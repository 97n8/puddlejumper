# Swanzey Capital Plan — Your Questions, Answered

*A plain-language FAQ for residents and officials. Every dollar figure is a planning estimate
calibrated to the Town's published totals; final numbers are confirmed with engineering, NHDOT/NHDES,
and DRA. Prepared as a companion to the Capital Forecast Book.*

---

**Q: What is this plan, in one sentence?**
A way to fund all five of the Town's big capital needs — the public works facility, the Christian Hill
Road bridge, Lower Winchester Street, the West Swanzey water system, and Town Hall accessibility — over
ten years, mostly with **state, federal, and water-rate money**, using **majority-vote savings funds
instead of bonds**, so there's **no tax spike and no failed 3/5 vote**.

**Q: Didn't we already vote down the public works facility? Twice?**
Yes — a $1.2M **bond** fell short of the **3/5 supermajority** it needed (678–485, then 681–485 on
recount). Here's the key point: a clear **majority of voters said yes.** The 3/5 rule, not the town's
will, stopped it. This plan doesn't re-fight that vote — it uses a **capital reserve fund**, which
needs only a **simple majority**, so the town can save up and build the facility **without a bond at
all.**

**Q: Isn't this just more spending?**
No — it's the *same* needs, paid for *smarter*. The town has to fix these things either way; deferring
them just makes them cost more. This plan actually **lowers the total cost** by capturing money we're
otherwise leaving on the table — like the **80% the state will pay** toward an eligible bridge.

**Q: How much of this do *we* actually pay?**
Of about **$5.3 million** in total needs, only about **$2.0 million is local** — roughly **38%.** The
other **62%** comes from outside: state bridge aid, a state water loan repaid by water rates, and
grants.

**Q: What does it do to my tax bill?**
On a **$300,000 home**, the *added* cost peaks at about **+$75 per year** for one year (2030), and is
**+$36 to +$66** in the other build years. Then — once everything is built — the capital part of your
bill drops to about **$18 *below* where it is today.** You pay a little more for about five years, then
less than now, with every project done.

**Q: Why capital reserve funds instead of a bond?**
| | Bond | Capital Reserve |
|---|---|---|
| Vote needed | **3/5 supermajority** | **Simple majority** |
| Interest cost | Yes | **None** |
| If it fails | Project dies | Plan just slows |
A reserve fund is a town savings account with a dedicated purpose. We set money aside each year and pay
cash. No borrowing, no interest, no supermajority.

**Q: Can the select board really spend that money without another town vote?**
Only because the warrant article naming the reserve also names the board as **"agents to expend"**
(RSA 35:15) — which **you** approve when you create the fund. It can only be spent on the **stated
purpose** (e.g., the public works facility), it's held by the elected **Trustees of Trust Funds**, and
it's fully audited. You authorize the savings account *and* its purpose up front.

**Q: What if the state bridge aid never comes?**
The bridge appropriation is written to be **contingent on receiving state aid** — so if the 80% doesn't
come through, the town isn't on the hook for the full project. Meanwhile we **bank our 20% share** in a
reserve so we're ready the moment aid is available. We lose nothing by being prepared.

**Q: Will this raise my water bill?**
The West Swanzey water work is funded through the state's **Drinking Water State Revolving Fund** — a
**1% loan repaid through water rates**, plus grants and possible loan forgiveness. That means it's paid
by the people who use the system, **not by everyone's property taxes.** Any rate change would come with
a separate, public rate study. *(If the system is a separate water precinct, that district — not the
Town — handles it.)*

**Q: Does this plan fight the people trying to save the Christian Hill Road bridge?**
No — it **helps** them. Applying for state bridge aid takes time and sits in a queue, which lines up
with the petition to **study the bridge for a year.** The plan makes sure the **money is ready** when
the town decides what to do. Same runway, not a fight.

**Q: What happens if voters reject one of the deposits one year?**
Nothing breaks. Each reserve deposit is its own simple-majority article. Skip a year and the plan
**slows down**; it never collapses the way a single failed bond does. That resilience is the whole
point.

**Q: Why should I trust these numbers?**
You shouldn't take them on faith — and you don't have to. Every figure is a **published planning
estimate**, sourced and labeled, and the final costs are confirmed by engineers, NHDOT/NHDES, and the
NH Department of Revenue Administration before anything is built. The **full Capital Forecast Book**,
with the math behind every number, is public.

**Q: What's the catch?**
Patience. The public works facility gets built around **2031**, not next year, because we save instead
of borrow. The trade for that patience: **no interest, no failed bond votes, no tax spike,** and a town
that **never again gets caught without a plan** for a big-ticket repair.

**Q: What is the town actually being asked to approve?**
A set of **capital reserve funds** (public works facility, bridges, roads, highway equipment, fire
apparatus, facilities, technology), each a simple-majority savings vote, plus **authorizations** to go
get the state and grant money. Year-one deposits total about **$520,000 — which unlocks millions in
outside funding.**

**Q: Where can I read the whole thing?**
The Capital Forecast Book, the ten-year numbers, and the model warrant articles are all public — on the
Town website and in the meeting materials. Bring your questions to the information night before the
deliberative session.

---

*The bottom line: we stop losing bond votes, we start banking the state's money, and we fix everything
— for the price of a tank of oil a year, briefly, then less than today.*
