## Section 2 — Project Description

**Executive Summary (≈250 words).** National Energy of Lincoln operates an 18 MW wood/wood-waste biomass power plant
in rural Alcona County — an EGLE-permitted industrial asset and ~21-job employer whose Consumers Energy PPA expires in
2027. Michigan's MPSC has already affirmed the plant's resource-adequacy value (Docket U-20496) by rejecting early
termination, yet the 2027 transition forces a concrete decision: modernize and decarbonize the facility's operations,
or let it become a stranded asset and decommission. This feasibility study evaluates a **deep-decarbonization
transition pathway** for the facility — combining **energy efficiency and process optimization, industrial
electrification of process/auxiliary heat, onsite solar-plus-storage, enabling grid/interconnection upgrades, and
substitution of an onsite-recovered syngas stream for fossil support fuel** — with the explicit goal of identifying at
least one pathway that reduces the facility's operational/fossil emissions by **50% or more** over time. An integrated
pyrolysis-based resource-recovery component (converting end-of-life tires, creosote-treated railroad ties, and
forestry residuals through partner National Salvage) is evaluated as a **circularity and fuel-switching** measure that
displaces fossil inputs and diverts ~15,000 tons/year from landfill — not as a carbon-credit play. The study quantifies
GHG impacts against an EPA-grounded baseline, runs scenarios across energy prices, policy, technology, and
interconnection conditions, and assesses workforce and community co-benefits for a disadvantaged rural community. The
result is an actionable transition decision package for the facility **and** a transferable model for the many
Michigan biomass facilities facing PPA expiration and stranded-asset risk.

**Background and Rationale (≈250 words).**
- **Facility today:** an existing, permitted 18 MW biomass generator (~85 GWh/yr; ~21 jobs) selling to Consumers
  Energy under a PPA expiring 2027. Emissions arise from wood combustion (largely biogenic) plus fossil/operational
  loads (startup/support fuel and auxiliary electricity) — the latter is the decarbonization target.
- **Barriers to date:** (1) **financial** — the 2027 PPA cliff removes the revenue certainty needed to justify capital
  for modernization, and feasibility analysis itself is not financeable pre-transition; (2) **infrastructure** — any
  electrification/onsite-renewable pathway depends on interconnection/grid upgrades not yet scoped; (3)
  **informational** — no rigorous study exists of how to decarbonize *this* combustion asset's operations rather than
  simply retire it.
- **The counterfactual:** without this grant, the most realistic 2027 trajectory is **decommissioning** — ~21 jobs and
  a piece of rural resource-adequacy infrastructure lost, with no decarbonization at all. This funding makes possible
  a rigorous, scenario-based feasibility analysis the facility cannot finance on its own pre-transition, on the
  timeline the PPA expiration demands — turning a likely shutdown into a credible deep-decarbonization decision.

**Project Model and Study Approach (≈550 words).**
- **Study structure (design logic).** Five linked analytical components: **(1) Baseline & screening** — establish the
  facility GHG and energy baseline (EPA FLIGHT/GHGRP or activity data + EPA factors) and screen candidate
  interventions; **(2) Technical & operational feasibility** — engineering assessment of each retained intervention
  against the host's process and site; **(3) Enabling-infrastructure analysis** — utility service, grid
  interconnection, and site electrical upgrades required for electrification/onsite renewables (often the binding
  constraint); **(4) Financial, rate & scenario modeling** — costs, payback, and how feasibility shifts under energy
  prices, policy, technology, and interconnection conditions, including the post-2027 market; **(5) Synthesis &
  decision package** — prioritized near- vs long-term pathways, a ≥50% pathway, and an actionable transition
  recommendation plus a transferable framework. Each component feeds the next: the baseline bounds what counts as
  "deep," the engineering filters what's viable, the infrastructure analysis re-prices it, and the scenario modeling
  shows what becomes viable under which conditions.
- **Interventions to be evaluated.** *Near-term:* energy efficiency / process optimization; substitution of
  onsite-recovered **syngas for fossil support/startup fuel**; onsite **solar + storage** for auxiliary load.
  *Forward-looking / conditional:* **industrial electrification of process heat**; integrated **pyrolysis-based
  resource recovery** as a circularity/fuel-switching measure (tires, railroad ties, forestry residuals → renewable
  fuel + recovered carbon that displace fossil inputs); **enabling grid/interconnection** upgrades. Selected because
  together they offer a credible path beyond incremental gains toward ≥50% operational decarbonization while extending
  the asset's productive life.
- **Scenario analysis.** Variables: natural-gas vs electricity price trajectories (the central driver of any
  electrification/fuel-switch economics); **federal/state tax-credit and incentive continuity** (post-OBBBA); grid
  interconnection availability/cost; technology cost and maturity; and post-2027 market/PPA-replacement conditions.
  Results structured for **both** the facility decision (which pathway, when) **and** state learning (what conditions
  unlock deeper reductions at facilities like this).
- **Facility engagement.** National Energy provides data access, operations/staff time, and decision-maker
  participation (Garrett Schopp) throughout; findings feed the live 2027 transition decision.
- **Partner roles.** National Salvage/National Energy — facility ops, data, decision; WasteWerx — process engineering;
  independent energy engineer — GHG/AVERT modeling; Acadia — financial/scenario/investment-readiness; PublicLogic —
  governance, GHG accounting, deliverables, and community/workforce analysis (Rothschild).
- **Community & workforce lens.** A community/workforce sub-analysis (≤5% subaward) assessing job preservation/quality,
  training needs, local air quality and public-health effects of each pathway in a disadvantaged rural community.
- **Statewide value.** A replicable transition-vs-retirement methodology for Michigan's biomass and small industrial
  combustion fleet facing PPA expiration.

**Facility Decision-Making Context (≈200 words).** The decision is immediate and concrete: as the **2027 Consumers
Energy PPA expires**, National Energy must decide whether to **modernize and decarbonize the Lincoln plant** (a
multi-million-dollar capital and operating-configuration decision) or **decommission** it. The decision-maker is
**Garrett Schopp** (Project Sponsor; National Salvage/National Energy), with authority to act on the study's
recommendations. The PPA expiration is itself the capital-planning trigger; equipment and interconnection decisions
must be made in the near term. Study results — a prioritized, costed, scenario-tested set of decarbonization pathways
and a ≥50% transition option — are designed to land **before** the transition is forced, so the facility can choose
modernization over retirement on evidence rather than default.

## Section 3 — Project Impacts and Objectives

**Barriers, Gaps, and Enabling Conditions (≈220 words).** The facility-specific barriers: **(1) Revenue-cliff
financing** — the 2027 PPA expiration removes the offtake certainty that would justify modernization capital, and
pre-transition feasibility work is itself unfundable without this grant; **(2) Interconnection/grid infrastructure** —
electrification and onsite-renewable pathways hinge on utility service and interconnection upgrades that have never
been scoped for this site; **(3) Rate/market structure** — post-PPA, the facility's economic basis shifts; the
relative price of electricity vs. fuel determines whether electrification/fuel-switching pays; **(4) Technology
maturity** — pyrolysis-based recovery and process electrification at this scale need engineering validation. The study
characterizes each gap and identifies the **enabling conditions** that would make currently-unfeasible pathways viable
— specific interconnection investments, incentive continuity, and price/market signals — generating exactly the kind
of "what would it take" insight the State can use across similar biomass assets. Understanding what is *not yet*
feasible at Lincoln, and why, is as valuable to Michigan as what is feasible today.

**Emissions Impact and Decarbonization Potential (≈240 words) — [30% of score; make this the strongest section].**
The study targets at least one pathway capable of reducing the facility's **operational/fossil emissions by ≥50%**
over time, combining electrification of process/auxiliary heat, onsite solar+storage, efficiency, and syngas
substitution for fossil support fuel. **Baseline:** drawn from **EPA FLIGHT/GHGRP** reported data if the facility
reports at threshold, otherwise an **activity-data estimate** from facility fuel/energy/electricity records and EPA
emissions factors, with **biogenic biomass CO₂ distinguished from fossil/operational emissions** so reduction claims
are credible. **Data/assumptions:** facility fuel and energy consumption, generation (~85 GWh/yr), auxiliary load, and
support-fuel records; Consumers Energy grid emissions factors. **Tools/methods:** **EPA's AVERT** for any
electricity-affecting intervention (electrification, onsite solar, storage); **EPA stationary-combustion emissions
factors** for fuel-switching and efficiency measures; AERMOD only if local air-quality analysis is warranted. Each
intervention gets a bounded GHG estimate (range/percentage), **near-term vs long-term distinguished**, with
uncertainties identified and tightened as the engineering and scenario work progresses. We will also quantify
**avoided landfill emissions** from the ~15,000 t/yr feedstock diversion as a lifecycle co-benefit. *(Note: this
program does not fund carbon-credit or offset-market analysis; recovered-carbon and fuel outputs are treated here only
as fossil-input-displacing circularity measures, not as tradable credits.)*

**Community Benefits and Co-Benefits (≈230 words).** Lincoln sits in rural Alcona County; the plant's ~21 jobs are a
material share of the local industrial base, and the 2027 cliff is a just-transition flashpoint. The study includes a
dedicated **community & workforce sub-analysis (community-partner subaward ≤5% of award)** assessing: **job
preservation and quality** (the ~21 existing positions and ~15 potential new operations/training roles), **workforce
skills/training needs** for a modernized facility, **local air-quality and public-health** effects of each
decarbonization pathway, and **local economic co-benefits** (supply-chain, local procurement, feedstock logistics via
National Salvage and Lake State Railway). A community partner/nonprofit `[CONFIRM partner — e.g., a local Alcona
County workforce or community organization; the network's ACS Cooperative/The Warehouse are Flint-based and better
suited to the Flint site]` conducts engagement and a community-action component tied directly to the facility's
pathways. Dr. Allison Weiss Rothschild (PsyD, LICSW, BCBA, LABA; implementation science / CFIR) leads this analysis,
ensuring workforce and community findings are integrated into the study design and the transition recommendation —
not bolted on. The aim is a quantified, facility-tied community-benefit method that strengthens the recommendation and
transfers to other rural Michigan facilities facing the same transition.

**Technology and Innovation Value (≈230 words).** The study evaluates an **underutilized combination** for small
biomass-combustion assets: pairing **industrial electrification + onsite renewables + efficiency** (to cut the
facility's operational emissions) with **integrated pyrolysis-based resource recovery** as a circularity/fuel-switching
measure that displaces fossil inputs and converts waste streams (end-of-life tires, creosote-treated railroad ties,
forestry residuals) into renewable fuel and recovered carbon. The forward-looking/conditional pathway — process
electrification plus pyrolysis-syngas substitution at an operating biomass plant — is technically interesting and
largely unstudied in Michigan. We articulate the **barriers** (interconnection cost, capital, pyrolysis emissions
permitting, technology maturity) and the **enabling conditions** (incentive continuity, grid upgrades, price signals)
that would move each from conditional to viable, and assess how **technology progress, policy, and market changes
shift feasibility and economics over time**. (Per program rules, carbon capture/storage is not evaluated as a
stand-alone intervention.) The innovation value is a validated template for converting a stranded-asset-bound biomass
plant into a modernized, lower-carbon industrial operation.

## Section 3 (cont.) — Replicability & Modernization

**Replicability and Value to Michigan (≈230 words).** Michigan has multiple small biomass and industrial-combustion
facilities approaching PPA expiration or stranded-asset decisions — National Salvage alone operates a second biomass
plant at **McBain (20 MW)**. The Lincoln study is explicitly designed to produce a **transferable
transition-vs-retirement methodology**: a replicable baseline-and-screening approach, an enabling-infrastructure
checklist, a scenario framework, and a workforce/community-benefit accounting method that other Michigan facilities
and the State can reuse. Findings can inform **state industrial-decarbonization strategy, utility interconnection and
rate program design, and future grant program structure** by clarifying what enabling conditions unlock deep
reductions at facilities like this. Beyond the required deliverables, the team commits to **active knowledge transfer**:
participation in at least one EGLE-hosted/-approved knowledge-sharing activity, plus targeted dissemination via the
**Michigan Manufacturers Association** and relevant bioenergy/industrial forums `[CONFIRM specific venues]`, a
public-facing case study/technical brief, and direct peer engagement with comparable Michigan biomass operators
(including McBain) to support replication. Multi-facility applicability — across the network's sites and the broader MI
biomass fleet — amplifies the study's statewide value.

**Facility Modernization and Long-Term Competitiveness (≈220 words).** The 2027 PPA expiration is precisely the
modernization-decision window that makes adopting the study's approaches realistic: capital and operating-configuration
choices are live, not hypothetical. A facility that would otherwise be decommissioned can instead become a modernized,
lower-carbon industrial operation with new workforce pathways. We expect the study to reveal what is **not yet well
understood**: whether process electrification + onsite renewables + pyrolysis-syngas substitution can credibly reach
≥50% operational decarbonization at an 18 MW biomass asset, what interconnection and capital thresholds gate it, and
under which price/policy scenarios it pencils. Those insights inform near-term investment at Lincoln and at comparable
Michigan facilities, and position the asset (and the rural community around it) to compete in the next industrial
economy rather than exit it.
