# PublicLogic — Business Model & Path

**The model: go after businesses, get the business to fund PublicLogic to build the town's capacity (systems +
trainings), so the business's development happens and a durable business–town partnership is created.**

A business-funded, public–private bridge. The payer is the private side; the beneficiary/partner is the town;
PublicLogic is the trusted builder that makes both sides work. Drawn from PublicLogic's own engagements (Shrewsbury
FY26 Municipal Fiber, Sutton) and the funding architecture in this folder. Figures are PublicLogic's data points and
planning assumptions, not quotes. Not legal/accounting advice.

---

## 1. The model in one sentence

**PublicLogic sells businesses the public-side capacity their development depends on — town governance systems and
staff trainings — funded by the business, so the development lands faster and cleaner and an ongoing business–town
partnership is created and stewarded.**

The insight: a town rarely has money or urgency. **A business with a development in (or near) that town has both.** So
you don't sell to the party that lacks budget — you sell to the party that needs the town to function and will pay to
make it so. The town gets capacity it could never have funded; the business gets a de-risked, accelerated, goodwill-
generating path; PublicLogic gets paid by the motivated party and keeps the durable town relationship (the retainer
tail).

## 2. Who pays — and why a business funds civic capacity

| Business / payer | Their development | Why they fund PublicLogic to work with the town |
|---|---|---|
| **Infrastructure / broadband / utility** (fiber, ISP, SELCO-type, energy) | A buildout that needs municipal governance, permitting, and staff who can run it | Trainings + governance let the town **adopt and operate** the buildout; without it the project stalls. *Shrewsbury Municipal Fiber is exactly this.* |
| **Real-estate / brownfield / mixed-use developer** | A project that needs site-readiness, permitting capacity, and a functioning town counterpart | Funds town capacity as a **project soft cost / site-readiness** spend — buys speed and predictability through approvals |
| **Tax-credit deal sponsors / investors** (NMTC, §45Q, OZ, LIHTC, Brownfields) | A capital deal needing a compliant, capable municipal partner | Funds PublicLogic's town-capacity + compliance work as a **deal cost** (this is Example B in the stack model) |
| **Govtech / SaaS / equipment vendors** | Selling a product towns can't absorb without help | Funds **adoption + training** so their product actually deploys — channel enablement, with PublicLogic as the trusted integrator |
| **Anchor institutions / large employers** (hospitals, universities, energy) | Community development, workforce, host-community goodwill | Funds trainings/capacity as a **community-benefit / partnership** vehicle |

What they're all really buying: **a town that can say yes, and then operate what it said yes to.** PublicLogic is how
the public side becomes a reliable counterparty.

## 3. What you deliver (to the town, paid by the business)

- **Stewardship Map** — diagnoses how the town actually operates and what's blocking the development/partnership.
- **VAULT + LogicOS / CivicPulse / PuddleJumper** — the town-configured governance systems the development needs to
  run on (modules mapped to statutory authority).
- **Trainings** — staff capacity so the town can *operate and sustain* what's built (the "etc." that makes adoption
  real — Rothschild's CFIR layer is what makes the training stick through turnover).
- **The partnership scaffolding** — the durable business–town relationship structure, stewarded over time.

**The client still owns everything at close.** No lock-in. That's what lets the *town* trust a business-funded
engagement — and what lets the *business* show a real, lasting community asset rather than a captured vendor.

## 4. How the money is structured (private capital, clean routes)

Because the payer is a business, the federal cost principles don't bind *its* payment to PublicLogic — but the
structure still has to be clean, especially where the town is a party. Routes:

- **Project soft cost / site-readiness** in the developer's or sponsor's own budget.
- **Tax-credit deal cost** — PublicLogic's town-capacity + compliance work as a project cost the investor/CDE funds
  (NMTC 7-yr compliance, §45Q, Brownfields — see `multiyear_funding_stack.xlsx`, Example B).
- **Community Benefit / Host-Community Agreement** — the developer's community-benefit obligation delivered as
  governance + training (a tangible, durable benefit) rather than cash.
- **Vendor channel-enablement / co-marketing** — the product vendor funds adoption + training.
- **Sponsorship / corporate-foundation / CSR** — for the workforce and partnership-building pieces.
- **PILOT / TIF / DIF offsets** — capacity-building funded inside the development's tax-incentive structure.

Where federal or town money *also* enters the stack, PublicLogic's fee still rides as an allowable professional
service (**2 CFR §200.459**) or a **MGL c.30B §7** sole-source services agreement — see
`sole_source_federal_overlay.md`. **But the lead dollar is the business's.**

## 5. The two-sided flywheel

> A **business** funds a cheap **Map** with a target town → the Map produces the **capacity plan + the development
> case** → PublicLogic **builds the town's systems and trains the staff** (business-funded) → the development **lands**
> and the **partnership** is set → the town becomes a **reference that attracts the next business**, and the business
> becomes a **reference that attracts the next town** → PublicLogic keeps the **stewardship retainer** on each town.

It compounds on **both** sides: every completed engagement is simultaneously a business reference and a town
reference, and each town added to the retainer base lowers the cost of serving the next (shared VAULT modules).

Real anchor: **Shrewsbury** — a fiber/infrastructure development where PublicLogic set up the governance and trainings
($123K implementation: $55K deploy + $42K code + $18K continuity + $8K training). Reframed to this model, the
*development side* funds that capacity so the buildout is adopted and operated — and the town relationship persists as
the tail.

## 6. The moat — now two-sided

You're the only integrated source (municipal policy + custom code by one person + CFIR behavioral science), **and**
you're trusted by towns. That second half is the new moat: a business can hire a lobbyist or a consultant, but it
**cannot buy a town's trust** — PublicLogic's town relationships and client-owns-everything ethic are what make a
business-funded engagement acceptable to the public side. The business pays you precisely *because* the town trusts
you and not them.

## 7. The load-bearing risk — independence and pay-to-play (design this in, or it sinks the model)

A business paying PublicLogic to shape a town's systems is, if structured carelessly, **capture** — and the optics
alone can end the practice. This is the single thing the model must engineer around:

- **The town's interest is the deliverable, not the business's.** PublicLogic's methodology serves the town's
  durability; if it ever bends to the payer, the moat (town trust) is gone.
- **Full disclosure, every time.** The town knows who is paying and why; documented, not implied. (Same discipline as
  the existing c.268A dual-role disclosure in the Shrewsbury/Sutton justifications, extended to "who funds this.")
- **PublicLogic never makes the town's decisions** — not procurement, not permitting, not award. It builds capacity;
  the town decides. Keep clear of MA c.268A/c.268B and gift/anti-bribery exposure.
- **Clean acceptance channel** — business funds via a transparent agreement; the town accepts capacity through proper
  channels (gift acceptance, CBA/HCA, or a normal services procurement), not a back door.
- **Client owns everything; no lock-in** — the structural proof that the town, not the payer, ends up in control.

Get this right and it's a strength (transparent, durable, everyone better off). Get it wrong once and the trust that
*is* the business doesn't come back.

## 8. Why it's durable

- **Deeper, less-volatile pool.** Private development capital and community-benefit dollars are a larger, more
  recurring pool than competitive federal grants (BRIC/SLCGP-class). You're funded by the motivated party.
- **Bedrock applied to your own revenue.** Your bedrock = **stewardship retainers** on the towns (own-source,
  multi-year). Business-funded builds are the **upside that opens new towns**; the retainer base is what you live on.
- **Countercyclical.** When grant money tightens, development still happens and businesses still need functioning town
  partners — arguably *more* so.

## 9. The honest constraints

1. **Independence risk** (Section 7) — the existential one.
2. **Founder-bound delivery** — the moat ("Boudreau writes the policy *and* the code") is also the bottleneck; the
   VAULT module library is what eventually lets configuration replace invention.
3. **Two-sided sales** — you must keep *both* a business pipeline and town trust warm; slower than one-sided selling,
   but far stickier once turning.
4. **Market concentration** — MA Town-Manager municipalities and the businesses that want into them.

## 10. How you get there — the path

**Phase 0 — Proof (now).** Shrewsbury + Sutton as named references; products and templates built; the
business-funded framing validated on at least one real development. **Done when:** you can tell a business "here's a
town we made ready, here's what it unlocked."

**Phase 1 — Pick the lead vertical and package it.** Choose the first business type to hunt (broadband/infrastructure
is the warm one — Shrewsbury proves it). Build the **business-facing offer**: "fund a Map + capacity build for your
target town; here's the cost, timeline, and what it de-risks." Standardize the **independence/disclosure structure**
(Section 7) so it's clean by default. *Metric: business meetings → funded Maps.*

**Phase 2 — Run lighthouse deals and prove the unlock.** Land 1–2 business-funded town engagements where the
development demonstrably moved *because* the capacity was built. Each yields a **business case study + a town
reference + reusable VAULT modules**. *Metric: development-unlocked stories; Map→Build conversion.*

**Phase 3 — Stack retainers (your bedrock).** Convert every town built into a **stewardship retainer**, so PublicLogic
carries a recurring base independent of the next business deal. *Metric: # towns on retainer; **% of revenue
recurring**; ARR.*

**Phase 4 — Scale by vertical and region, break the founder bottleneck.** Repeat the packaged offer across verticals
(broadband → developers → tax-credit sponsors → vendors) and regions; mature the **module library** so delivery is
configuration not invention; let CaseSpace/PuddleJumper host many towns and Rothschild's CFIR layer compound renewals.
*Then* a second practitioner / "PublicLogic-inside" model becomes possible. *Metric: delivery-hours per new town (must
fall); businesses-per-vertical; renewal rate.*

## 11. The model on one line

**Sell businesses the town-readiness their development depends on → let the business fund the Map, the build, and the
trainings → keep the town relationship as a stewardship retainer → reuse the module library so each new
business-funded town costs less to serve than the last —** all while the town owns everything and decides everything,
which is the only reason a business-funded civic engagement is allowed to exist.
