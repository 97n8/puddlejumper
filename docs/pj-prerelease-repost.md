Someone in an interview asked me what I wanted to be when I grew up.

I'm running a govtech company and a state rep campaign in the 2nd Worcester District at the same time. I've got an MPA, a MCPPO, and fifteen years of municipal scars. And they hit me with the kindergarten question.

I didn't have a good answer when I was five, either.

I never wanted to be a firefighter or an astronaut. I wanted to know who decided where the stop sign went. Not the engineer who designed it. The person who said *here*.

I fell into municipal government by showing up. Committee member. City Councillor. Town Administrator. And in every role, in every town, the same thing: good people drowning in bad process. A resident gets transferred three times for a permit. A department head stays late because the approval chain lives in somebody's head and that somebody's on vacation. A clerk keeps a spreadsheet that is, functionally, the law — and nobody knows it exists until she retires. The names change. The org charts change. The problem never does.

We ask people to carry loads the structure should carry. Then we blame them when they drop it.

That's what I wanted to be when I grew up. I just didn't have the language for it yet. The person who fixes the invisible machinery — not the trucks and pipes and roads, but the rules about who can approve what, the process that determines whether the thing you need actually reaches the person who can do something about it.

I wrote a framework called VAULT. Verification, Authority, Utility, Legitimacy, Transfer. Five conditions that have to be true before a government action should happen. I knew it was right the way you know load-bearing walls — you feel them. But it was just a framework on paper until my co-founder Allison — Dr. Allison Weiss Rothschild, organizational psychologist — made it real. She sees the behavioral fault lines I'd walk right past. I wrote the doctrine. She turned it into something that actually changes how people work.

So we built PuddleJumper. The first governance process runtime — a layer that sits between a decision and an action, evaluates authority, routes to the right person, and produces an audit trail that can't be altered. You can't bypass the record. It's not a feature. It's the environment.

The logo is a duck in a WWII fighter plane. Calm on the surface, governance machinery underneath. That's the whole pitch.

We're live in Phillipston. Deploying in Sutton and Westminster on Community Compact IT grants. Running NMTC compliance for a ten-million-dollar biochar deal. I wrote the code myself. I wrote the grant applications myself. I write everything myself because if you can't explain it in plain language, you don't understand it well enough to build it.

And now I'm running for State Rep — Gardner, Ashburnham, Templeton, Winchendon — because I learned something in all those years: you can write the best software in the world and it still can't fix a statute. Some problems need someone in the room where the law gets made who's actually sat in the chair where the law gets applied.

So what do I want to be when I grow up?

The same thing I've always wanted to be. The person who makes the thing work the way it was supposed to work in the first place — and who's honest about the fact that right now, it doesn't.

PuddleJumper is in pre-release. This is why.
