## 1. The simple explanation

PublicLogic is building a governed execution platform for municipal work.

Most software gives a town another screen, inbox, chatbot, or workflow tool. PublicLogic adds the missing control layer underneath: a place where forms, files, approvals, AI assistance, records, and outside systems move through a known process with policy, permission, audit, and continuity built in.

The platform has three major surfaces:

1. **LogicOS** — where people work.
2. **PuddleJumper** — where governed actions execute.
3. **VAULT / ARCHIEVE** — where authority, evidence, and the record are preserved.

The important idea is simple:

> Users should not have to understand the infrastructure. They should be able to start work, move a case forward, and trust that the system keeps the record, applies the rules, and does not lose institutional memory.

## 3. What makes this different

PublicLogic is not just a chatbot, form builder, document repository, or dashboard.

The distinctive layer is the governed runtime:

```text
Request → Evaluate → Register → Approve → Authorize → Dispatch → ARCHIEVE
```

That means an action can be checked before it happens, tied to a policy or authority source, approved when needed, executed through a controlled connector, and preserved afterward.

This is the difference between:

- “AI did something”
- and
- “A governed system evaluated, authorized, executed, and preserved the action.”

For municipal work, that distinction matters.

---

## 4. The operating thesis

Municipal governments do not mainly need more apps. They need continuity.

Turnover, fragmented files, inbox-based work, inconsistent forms, unclear approvals, and disconnected vendor systems create risk. PublicLogic turns those pieces into structured CaseSpaces and governed flows that survive staff changes and preserve the record.

The product promise:

> Smart systems. Stronger communities. Work that holds when people change.

---

## 6. Product boundary

LogicOS and LogicCommons are browser-facing work surfaces. They are not supposed to become uncontrolled token holders or direct execution layers.

PuddleJumper is the runtime. It holds provider tokens server-side, brokers privileged actions, and enforces the governance pipeline.

VAULT is the policy and authority layer.

ARCHIEVE is the final evidence and audit lane.

The boundary rule:

> Browser surfaces request work. PuddleJumper governs execution. VAULT authorizes. ARCHIEVE preserves.

## 7. What to sell first

The strongest first product is not “the whole platform.”

The strongest first offer is a narrow governed CaseSpace where the pain is obvious:

- Public records requests
- Permits and licenses
- Clerk workflows
- Board compliance
- Capital projects
- Grant execution
- Procurement packets
- Property / Airbnb / small-business operating files

Each use case should be packaged as:

```text
Start here → intake → CaseSpace → governed flow → record package → continuity
```

That is the repeatable product.
